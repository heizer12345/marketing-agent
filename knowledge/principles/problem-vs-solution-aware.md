# Problem-Aware vs Solution-Aware copy

The single most common copy mistake: writing solution-aware copy for a problem-aware audience (or vice versa). Different temperatures → different copy → different landing pages.

## Problem-Aware copy
- The reader feels the pain but doesn't yet know solutions exist.
- Lead with the pain in their words.
- Don't introduce competitor names or comparisons.
- Goal: get them to believe a category of solution exists.

**Hook examples**:
- "Tired of Alibaba RFQs going nowhere?"
- "Most importers waste their first month chasing supplier replies."

## Solution-Aware copy
- The reader knows solutions exist. They're comparing.
- Lead with positioning vs alternatives, or proof.
- Use specific competitor names where it's legal and accurate.
- Goal: convince them you're the right solution.

**Hook examples**:
- "Sourcy vs Alibaba: managed sourcing in 3 hours, not 3 weeks."
- "Why 390 brands switched from freelance sourcing agents to Sourcy."

## Why not mix them
- A problem-aware reader sees a competitor comparison and feels lost ("I haven't even decided I want a tool yet").
- A solution-aware reader sees the pain pitch and feels condescended to ("I know I have this problem, what's different about YOU?").
- Mismatch = bounce.

## Rules of thumb
- Cold ads to broad audiences → almost always problem-aware copy.
- Retargeting + pricing page traffic → solution-aware copy.
- If you can only have one landing page, default to problem-aware copy (broader audience). Build the solution-aware variant when paid traffic justifies it.

## Template prompt
"Write {channel} copy for a {problem-aware|solution-aware} audience. ICP: {icp}. {Problem-aware: lead with pain {pain}. Don't mention competitors.} {Solution-aware: lead with comparison vs {competitor}. Lean on proof: {proof}.} Sourcy voice."
